One of my friends recently convinced me to start playing Magic the Gathering by giving me a Doctor Who set they got as part of a pack. While I really love my current deck, I am curious what the larger world of magic cards looks like. There are a few key components I know matter:

  • Mana cost: This is how much mana you have to pay to play a card or use one of its abilities. Mana costs can consist of any combination of colored and colorless mana1.
    • This analysis focuses on converted mana cost, which does not take into consideration the color of mana required.
  • Toughness: This is analogous to defense or hit points in other games.
  • Power: This is how hard a creature hits / how much damage it does on a hit.
  • Keywords: There are certain words that stand for common actions.
    • For example, haste means a creature can attack on its first turn, while flying means it cannot be blocked except by another creature with flying or reach.
  • Type: These affect how a card is played. For example, creatures are usually placed on your field and last until they die or an effect makes them leave while sorceries are typically only useful for one turn.
    • They can consist of a long string of types and interact with the effects of other cards, for example one of my favorite cards, Dinosaurs on a Spaceship, is a Creature - Dinosaur. So it’s affected by anything that targets creatures or dinosaurs (and one of its abilities is that it gives other dinosaurs a boost).

Each mana color in Magic the Gathering has its own general philosophy and play style it is expected to generally follow. A description for each will be on the relevant tab.

Overall questions:

  • Do the flavor text and keywords match the description of the colors?
  • What is most important in predicting converted mana cost of creatures and artifacts?

  • Converted mana cost is strongly skewed count data2, so regression yielded poor results

  • Of a few initial models, categorizing mana cost by high, medium, and low yielded the best overall accuracy

    • While it increased accuracy by 15.4% compared to a baseline model and is therefore useful, there is still room for improvement with an accuracy of 65.07%
    • The next step will likely be to include common keywords and go talk to a Subject Matter Expert3 about a better way to categorize mana cost

  • Overall, the flavor text of the different colors seems to match the general philosophy of the color archetypes4, but it’s a little difficult to judge this because a decent number of the words appear to be magic specific 5, so it adds a fair amount of noise.

  • The keywords also seem to match the general archetypes, but given how often the same keywords are important to different colors keywords alone likely don’t capture differences in playstyles. Ultimately, you can probably create a deck to fit nearly any playstyle using any combination of colors, it’s just a matter of how much effort will be required.

Description of Color

“Red represents freedom, chaos, fury, and warfare, pulling its power from mountains. Its powers are associated with the classical fire and earth elements, and tends to have the strongest spells such as fireballs that can be powered-up by tapping additional mana when cast. Red is an offense-oriented class: in addition to powerful creatures like dragons, red planeswalkers can summon weak creatures that can strike quickly to gain the short-term edge.” 10

Blue’s magic is also associated with control, allowing the player to gain temporary or full control of the opponent’s creatures. Blue creatures often tend to be weak but evasive and difficult to target.” 12

Description of Color

Colorless doesn’t have much of an identity or philosophy, and frequently appears alongside other colors. However, colorless cards do tend to fall into three categories: the artificial, the unknowable, and the underdeveloped.15 They do tend to be good for general use to support any play style, in part because they’re easier to add to any deck.
